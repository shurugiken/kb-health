# Writing Rules: Anti-AI-Slop Style Guide

This file is a constraint set. Read it before writing anything. Every rule below bans a documented AI writing pattern. Violating these rules produces text that reads as machine-generated, promotional, or empty.

---

## 1. Puffery and Peacock Words

**What to ban:** Words and phrases that assert greatness without evidence.

| Banned | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| `boasts` | Promotional. Says "we're bragging" instead of stating a fact. |
| `nestled` | Travel-brochure filler. Never describes anything real. |
| `breathtaking`, `stunning`, `remarkable` | Subjective superlatives with no argumentative content. |
| `rich tapestry`, `rich heritage`, `rich history` | Hollow intensifier on "tapestry/heritage/history." |
| `vibrant` | Default AI adjective for any community or culture. |
| `profound` | Tells the reader how to feel rather than giving them reason to feel it. |
| `groundbreaking`, `revolutionary`, `cutting-edge` | Press-release language. Reserve only when a specific, verifiable first is being named. |
| `renowned`, `esteemed`, `distinguished`, `celebrated` | Asserts notability; does not establish it. |
| `diverse array`, `wide array`, `wide range` | Vague quantity claim. Name the things instead. |
| `natural beauty` | Subjective and meaningless without context. |
| `in the heart of` | Geographic filler borrowed from real-estate copy. |
| `commitment to`, `dedication to` | Claims values without evidence of actions. |
| `exemplifies`, `embodies`, `epitomizes` | Rhetorical assertion substituting for demonstration. |

**Rewrite rule:** State the specific fact that would make the reader form the positive judgment themselves. Never tell them what to conclude.

- BAD: `The city boasts a vibrant arts scene.`
- GOOD: `The city has four independent art museums and more than sixty galleries.`

## 2. Significance Inflation ("Stands As a Testament")

**What to ban:** Generic statements declaring that something is important, enduring, or meaningful.

| Banned construction | Rewrite |
|---|---|
| `stands as a testament to` | Delete. State what the thing is or does. |
| `serves as a reminder that` | Delete. State the fact directly. |
| `serves as a symbol of` | Name what it symbolizes by describing what it does. |
| `plays a vital/pivotal/crucial/significant/key role` | State the role specifically. What does it do, exactly? |
| `underscores the importance of` | Delete. State the importance directly. |
| `reflects broader` | Name the broader thing being reflected. |
| `symbolizing its enduring/lasting legacy` | Drop entirely. If the legacy is real, name its effects. |
| `indelible mark on` | State what changed as a result. |
| `marks a turning point`, `marks a shift` | State what specifically changed and when. |
| `contributing to a better understanding of` | Delete. State what was understood, and by whom. |
| `setting the stage for` | State what followed. |
| `shaping the course of` | Name the course. Name the specific mechanism. |
| `focal point of` | State what the thing does, not that it is a "focal point." |
| `deeply rooted in` | Specify the historical or cultural grounding. |
| `evolving landscape` | Say what is changing and how. |

**Rewrite rule:** Every claim of significance must be cashed out in specific facts. "X plays a pivotal role in Y" is never acceptable. "X does Z, which causes Y to happen" is acceptable.

## 3. The "Not Just X, But Y" Construction

**What to ban:** Negative-parallelism framing that frames a correction the reader never needed.

- `Not just X, but also Y`
- `It's not just X — it's Y`
- `Not X, but Y`
- `It's not about X; it's about Y`
- `Not merely X, but truly Y`
- `Not only does it X, but it also Y`

**Why it fails:** This construction fabricates a misconception in order to "correct" it. It creates the impression of nuance while delivering none. It is among the most reliable markers of AI-generated motivational or explanatory prose.

**Rewrite rule:** Make the positive claim directly. If a genuine misconception exists, quote or cite it, then refute it with evidence.

- BAD: `It's not just a platform — it's a community.`
- GOOD: `The platform's moderation model is built around user-elected councils rather than centralized staff decisions.`

---

## 4. Rule-of-Three Overuse

**What to ban:** Reflexive stringing of exactly three items — adjectives, nouns, or clauses — to simulate thoroughness.

- `efficient, scalable, and reliable`
- `thoughtful, dedicated, and passionate`
- `innovation, collaboration, and excellence`
- Three-clause sentences where one or two would carry the meaning

**Why it fails:** The rule of three is a real rhetorical device, but AI applies it indiscriminately to every sentence, making it a tic rather than a tool. It creates the appearance of comprehensive coverage with zero additional information.

**Rewrite rule:** List items only when each item is independently meaningful. If two of the three are obvious or redundant, cut them. If you have more than three, list them all or pick the most consequential one.

## 5. Em-Dash Overuse

**What to ban:** Using `—` as a catch-all for parenthetical insertions, emphasis, or list-style elaboration.

- `The system — designed by engineers over three years — integrates seamlessly.`
- `There's one key insight here — trust matters.`
- `The results were clear — participation increased by 40%.`

**Why it fails:** Em dashes are a legitimate punctuation mark, but AI uses them as a substitute for structure. They appear multiple times per paragraph, flattening prose into a series of dramatic-pause fragments.

**Rewrite rule:** Use em dashes at most once per paragraph, only when you need a sharp interruption or a trailing elaboration that does not fit comfortably in parentheses. For everything else: restructure the sentence, use a period, or use a comma.

## 6. Present-Participle Comma Run-Ons

**What to ban:** Appending a participial phrase to the end of a sentence to imply a logical connection that does not exist.

| Banned pattern | Rewrite |
|---|---|
| `..., highlighting the importance of X.` | State the importance directly in the prior sentence. |
| `..., underscoring a commitment to Y.` | Delete or make a direct claim. |
| `..., reflecting broader trends in Z.` | State the trend. |
| `..., demonstrating that...` | Use a new sentence with a causal claim. |
| `..., emphasizing the need for...` | State the need as a direct assertion. |
| `..., showcasing its capabilities.` | Say what the capabilities are. |
| `..., fostering a sense of community.` | State what actually happened. |
| `..., paving the way for...` | State what follows. |
| `..., cementing its place as...` | Delete. |
| `..., ensuring that...` | State the outcome as a direct fact. |

**Why it fails:** These phrase-appendages simulate analytical commentary without providing any. They're the prose equivalent of shrugging while gesturing meaningfully.

**Rewrite rule:** If the participial phrase adds new information, make it a new sentence with an explicit subject. If it only restates the prior sentence, delete it.

## 7. Vague Attribution ("Experts Say")

**What to ban:** Citing unnamed authorities to give the impression of external validation.

| Banned | Rewrite |
|---|---|
| `industry experts say` | Name the expert and the source. |
| `studies suggest` | Cite the study. |
| `observers have noted` | Name the observers or delete the claim. |
| `analysts believe` | Name the analysts and their publication. |
| `some critics argue` | Name who, or cut the claim. |
| `according to several sources` | Name them, or state the fact without attribution. |
| `reports indicate` | Which reports? Cite them. |
| `research shows` | Cite the research or restate the fact as your own claim. |
| `experts agree` | Name them. "Experts" is not a source. |

**Rewrite rule:** Either name the specific source or make the claim as your own statement and stand behind it. Unnamed attribution is not hedging — it is fabrication.

## 8. Overused "AI Vocabulary" Words

Ban these words in their typical AI-context usage. Each one has a legitimate specific meaning; do not use them as filler or intensifiers.

### Tier 1 — Do not use at all in most contexts:

| Word | The problem |
|---|---|
| `delve` | Nearly never used in natural human writing. Use "examine," "explore," "look at," or name the specific action. |
| `tapestry` | Default AI metaphor for any complex topic. Meaningless. Delete. |
| `testament` | See Section 2. Never use in the construction "stands as a testament to." |
| `nestled` | Geographic cliche. State the actual location. |
| `interplay` | Vague relational term. Describe the actual relationship. |
| `intricacies`, `intricate` | Signals complexity without conveying any. Say what is complex and why. |
| `meticulous`, `meticulously` | Almost always redundant. If care was taken, describe it. |
| `garner` | Formal-sounding synonym for "get" or "attract." Use the plain word. |
| `underscore` (as verb) | See Section 2 and 6. Rarely necessary; almost always a filler word. |
| `bolstered` | Use "increased," "strengthened," or the specific mechanism. |

### Tier 2 — Use only with precision:

| Word | Rule |
|---|---|
| `crucial` | Only use if failure to do this thing causes a specific, named bad outcome. Not a general intensifier. |
| `essential` | Same as `crucial`. Not a synonym for "important." |
| `robust` | Specific engineering/systems term. Do not apply to generic positive things ("robust community"). |
| `seamless` | Specific UX claim. Do not use as marketing filler. If it is seamless, say why: no extra steps, no re-authentication, etc. |
| `leverage` (verb) | Use "use," "apply," or "rely on." "Leverage" as a verb is business-jargon filler in most contexts. |
| `pivotal` | Reserve for moments with a specific, demonstrable before/after change. |
| `vital` | Reserve for things where the absence causes failure. Not an intensifier. |
| `key` | Overused as a general intensifier. Use only when you mean "the one thing that unlocks the rest." |
| `landscape` | Use only as a literal noun. Not as in "the regulatory landscape" or "navigate the landscape." |
| `innovative` | Never use as a self-description or generic qualifier. Name the specific innovation. |
| `transformative` | Name the transformation: what was before, what is after. |

## 9. Prepositional Filler Phrases

**What to ban:** Phrase templates that pad sentences and signal AI formality.

| Banned phrase | Rewrite |
|---|---|
| `in the realm of` | Replace with "in" or restructure: "in cryptography" not "in the realm of cryptography." |
| `navigate the landscape` | Say what decisions or obstacles are actually involved. |
| `in the context of` | Usually deletable. If it isn't, use "in" or "when discussing." |
| `when it comes to` | Delete. Restructure the sentence. |
| `with regard to`, `with respect to` | Use "on," "about," or "for." |
| `in terms of` | Almost always removable. Restructure. |
| `at the end of the day` | Delete. |
| `it is worth noting that` | Delete. If it is worth noting, note it. |
| `it is important to note that` | Delete. Same reason. |
| `it goes without saying` | Then do not say it. Delete. |
| `needless to say` | Delete. |
| `first and foremost` | Delete. Say what comes first. |

## 10. Hollow Openings and Conclusions

**What to ban:** Structural boilerplate that marks the beginning and end of AI-generated sections.

### Banned openers:
- `In today's rapidly changing world,`
- `In an era of X,`
- `Throughout history,`
- `Since the dawn of X,`
- `As technology continues to evolve,`
- `As we navigate an increasingly X world,`

### Banned closers:
- `In conclusion,`
- `In summary,`
- `To summarize,`
- `Overall,`
- `Ultimately,` (when used as a closing throat-clear)
- `In short,`
- `All in all,`
- Restatements of the prior two paragraphs under a "Conclusion" heading that add nothing

**Rewrite rule:** Conclusions should contain the single most important thing — either a judgment, a recommendation, or the finding the whole piece was building toward. If a summary section only repeats what was already said, delete it. If an opening sentence does not contain information, delete it and start with the first real claim.

## 11. The "Challenges and Future Prospects" Formula

**What to ban:** Formulaic two-part section structure that ends every piece or entry with optimistic hedging.

- Section heading `Challenges and Future Prospects` or `Challenges and Legacy` or `Future Outlook`
- Pattern: "Despite its successes, X faces several challenges including A, B, and C. However, with growing interest and continued investment, X is well-positioned to..."
- Unsubstantiated speculation about potential initiatives or future directions
- Vaguely positive endings that do not commit to any specific claim

**Rewrite rule:** Do not write a "future" section unless you are reporting on a specific announced plan or a concrete trend with data behind it. Do not manufacture optimism. If challenges exist, name them specifically; do not use them as a setup for a "but the future looks bright" reversal.

---

## 12. Title Case and Heading Bloat

**What to ban:**
- Title-casing every word in a section heading: `Impact of Technology and Digitalization on Modern Society`
- Creating a heading for every paragraph
- Heading names that are vague label-words: "Overview," "Background," "Additional Information," "More Details"
- Skipping heading levels (H2 directly to H4)

**Rewrite rule:** Use sentence case for headings. Use headings only to mark genuine section breaks where a reader might want to navigate directly. "Overview" is almost never a useful heading — the opening paragraph is already the overview.

---

## 13. Copula Avoidance

**What to ban:** Replacing "is/are/has" with ornate equivalents that signal formal register without adding meaning.

| Banned | Use instead |
|---|---|
| `serves as` | `is` |
| `stands as` | `is` |
| `marks` (when meaning "is") | `is` |
| `represents` (when meaning "is") | `is` |
| `boasts` (when meaning "has") | `has` |
| `features` (when meaning "has/includes") | `has`, `includes` |
| `maintains` (when meaning "has") | `has` |
| `offers` (when meaning "has/provides") | `has`, `provides` |
| `refers to` (in definitions where "is" works) | `is` |

**Why it fails:** AI models are trained on patterns that penalize repetition of simple words. The result is that every "is" gets decorated into a construction that sounds more important but is harder to read. Use plain copulas. Clarity is not weakness.

---

## 14. Lexical Variety as a Tell

**What to ban:** Excessive synonym substitution to avoid repeating the same word.

AI models operate under a repetition penalty that causes them to rotate through synonyms unnecessarily. Human writers repeat important words intentionally for coherence.

**Pattern examples:**
- A person referred to as "the individual," "the subject," "the figure," "the personality," and "the persona" within one paragraph.
- A product described as a "tool," "solution," "platform," "system," and "offering" in consecutive sentences when they all mean the same thing.

**Rewrite rule:** Repeat the key noun when that noun is the subject of the piece. If "system" is the right word, use "system." Only substitute when precision requires a different word — not to avoid repetition.

---

## 15. Inline Bold Abuse

**What to ban:** Boldfacing terms throughout body text in the style of a listicle or README.

- `This is a **key** consideration.`
- `The **most important** step is initialization.`
- Bolding the first two words of every bullet in a list

**Rewrite rule:** Bold is for terms being formally defined for the first time in a technical document, or for genuinely critical warnings. It is not emphasis, it is not decoration, and it is not a substitute for strong sentence construction.

## Summary of Core Rules

1. State facts. Do not assert that facts are important, meaningful, or transformative — the facts speak.
2. Name sources. "Experts" and "studies" are not citations.
3. Use plain verbs. "Is" and "has" are stronger than "serves as" and "boasts."
4. One em dash per paragraph, maximum.
5. Cut any sentence that ends with a participial phrase ("..., highlighting X").
6. Never open with a "today's world" frame. Never close with "In conclusion."
7. Cut any section whose heading is "Challenges and Future Prospects" or equivalent.
8. Repeat important nouns. Synonym-rotating for "clarity" destroys coherence.
9. Three-item lists are a reflex, not a structure. Audit every one.
10. The "not just X, but Y" frame is always deletable. Delete it.
